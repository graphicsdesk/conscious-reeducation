Overview

As we watch the latest public outrage stemming from the murder of George Floyd, Breonna Taylor, and Ahmaud Arbery, some may find themselves experiencing dissonance that they had been so unaware of the history of policing, plantation capitalism, and racist government policies created with the intent to limit Black progress. The American education system does not prioritize the teaching of the enslavement period, or the idea of race and racism as evidenced by the lack of knowledge surrounding the economic and political achievements of communities of color. Many of us navigate the Pre-K-12 school experience, which includes state mandated assessments and honors and AP courses, and never learn about black wall street, black inventions, theories. The end result is that many of us go through our school careers racially illiterate, sensitive to the stress of conversations about race, and ignorant that our thoughts and behaviors fuel propagate white supremacy, even the most well-meaning amongst us. The reality is that skin color and zip code shape every experience in this country and act as determining factors for quality of life and life expectancy for those yet to be born. The solution is to unlearn the whitewashed curriculum we were immersed in for most of our lives and relearn the history of our founding through a conscious lens to truly understand what is happening in the streets and finally take anti-racist actions.

Claim 1: Structural racism goes deeper than you think (why structural racism is overlooked and how important is it reteach history as it directly affects how issues of race are perceived now)

The roots of race and racism in America go back to 1619 when that first ship arrived in the colony of Virginia. Without an understanding of what happened during that time period, you are unable to perceive systemic racism as a system of gears that act upon every aspect of society. Unless you can take this critical step back to see the entire system, you cannot recognize that the ways in which we’ve been socialized within American society has entrenched white supremacy deep into our collective psyche.

Consider this: A well-meaning teacher, statistically a white woman, stands in front of a classroom filled with students of color. She tries to encourage them, urge them to do better, to graduate, enroll in college, so they could live in a better neighborhood. Unbeknownst to the teacher, a “better neighborhood” is subconsciously equated with a white neighborhood. “A better school district” usually corresponds with a whiter school district and so on. We already speak in the language of race everyday, why not do so from a point of consciousness so that we can truly understand our impact and how it aids in white supremacy. By exploring the resources provided throughout this project, you can start to build your knowledge and empathy of the history of this country, which was founded upon the social construction of race.

Claim 2: Better understanding your individual's experiences contextualize racism? (why looking to more individualistic experiences with race can aid you in contextualizing yourself within it whether you are white or black?)

In his work, Courageous Conversations about Race by Glenn E. Singleton, he urges us to examine race through the personal, local, and immediate. This forces us to understand our own beliefs, values, and expectations about race. This helps us to think more critically and empathetically about how we show up not only in the world, but any given space that we occupy. In American society today, it is difficult for us to connect our privileges, however it manifests, to the oppression of communities of color that are invisible to us because of historically redlined neighborhoods and present day discrimination within real estate. Even more, we buy into the idea that our privileges are earned due to “hard work” rather than critically interrogating the impact of generational wealth being as a result of stolen labor and racist government policies designed to limit the progress of communities of color. Our lack of exposure makes it easy to buy into the idea of “meritocracy,” while missing that this fictional narrative is a tool of white supremacy. As we question the qualifications of people of color in our school and workplaces, using affirmative action to maintain a sense of superiority, we must also recognize that the historic structures within American society have rooted affirmative action for white people in every aspect of life. As we consider our own experiences, studying the experiences of others helps us to better see how our false notions coincide with someone else’s oppression.

The idea of revolution might be a frightening thought to anyone who has benefited from the racial caste system in America, but simply told, this country was born from Revolution. Revolution against British policing of the American colonies led to modern day America. Revolution against French colonialism in Haiti led to the first Black-led republic. Revolution in South Africa led to the end of Apartheid. While we may look unfavorably at the practices of revolution when we apply the lens of race in this country, it has long been a vehicle for much of the progress we have made as a society, which everyone benefits from. Black people are 13% of the population but have consistently forced America to actualize its founding promises. As long as the conditions that caused the American Revolution are still in existence, the need for revolution remains just as strong today.

Conclusion

Unlearning is the first step in our collective liberation, but it is not the final step. We have been socialized in a system that privileges racial illiteracy. You can reach the highest office of this land without demonstrating a true understanding of race and racism or how policies continue to exacerbate the racial wealth gap. You can stand before students of color and measure their intelligence through how well they can recite a history that is not their own using a colonial language not spoken by their ancestors. Neutrality is not an option in a country founded on genocide and the enslavement of millions. Our options are, and have always been, furthering racism or anti-racism. Engaging in a conscious learning, and corroboration, of what you know about the founding of America is a civic responsibility and the only way to become truly anti-racist. Anti-racism work is difficult, and painful, but so too is the history of marginalized communities in America who will not be free until white people free themselves from the prison of white supremacy.
